Seaport continues to expand despite increasing threat of rising sea

By: Maria Bonnemaison

Boston, MA -- The new and trency neighbordhood of Seaport was built on what was once an empty lot along the Boston Harbor. Today you'll find brand new sky rises filled with corporate offices and luxury apartments, along with bustling construction sites scattered throughout the neighborhood.

Many development projects are wrapping up their construction, while others are preparing to being after receiving approval from the city. Seaport is still growing but so is the threat of flooding from rising sea-levels. What is now considered Boston's modern and affluent neighborhood is also most at risk to suffer the effects of climate change.

A new report from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration predicts that by the year 2050, the sea level in Boston will be 16 inches higher than it was at the start of the 21st century. By 2030, the sea level is projected to rise 9 inches above what they were in 2013. Coastal areas like South Boston Waterfront are the most vulnerable to the effect of climate change and rising seas, but these effects can already be seen.

In 2018, a bomb cyclone mixed with high tide caused significant flooding along the waterfront. In the past few years, king tides have become more frequent causing flooding in the area.

But as the threats of climate change grow, the Seaport has made little plans to combat the threats. Boston city official Mariama White-Hammond, chief of environment, energy, and open spaces, has expressed urgency to execute a plan to protect Seaport from severe flooding events.

In the meantime, multiple development projects have been approved and begun construction, like the 23-acre Harbor Square Park and 10 World Trade, a state-of-the-art research and technology facility.
